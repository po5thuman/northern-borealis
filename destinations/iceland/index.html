There's nothing quite like watching the aurora borealis over Iceland for the first time. The Best Place and Time to See the Northern Lights in Iceland

These are the two questions every trip to see the aurora borealis over Iceland ultimately comes down to: where do you stand, and when do you go? Here's the honest answer to both.

Best Place to See the Northern Lights in Iceland

The single biggest factor in whether you see the lights isn't luck — it's light pollution. Anywhere with a clear, dark view away from town and city lights gives you a real chance. In practice, that means:

Reykjavik is the easiest base logistically. It's not the darkest spot in the country on its own, but a short 20–40 minute drive gets you well clear of the city glow, and the sheer number of tours running nightly makes it the most flexible option.

Akureyri and North Iceland are genuinely among the best spots to see the aurora borealis in the whole country — remote, low light pollution, and surrounded by open countryside and fjords. If a great photo matters as much as the sighting itself, this is where to go.

Other well-known spots — the Snaefellsnes Peninsula, Thingvellir National Park, and the south coast near Vik — are also excellent, and are usually included as stops on multi-hour guided tours rather than somewhere you'd base yourself for the whole trip.

Best Time to See the Northern Lights in Iceland

Iceland's northern lights season runs from late August to mid-April — essentially, whenever the nights are dark enough. Within that window, the best time to visit Iceland for the northern lights is late September through March, when darkness sets in early enough each evening to make a tour practical. November, December and January have the longest nights and the best overall odds, though they also bring the coldest weather and shortest daylight hours for sightseeing.

If you're weighing up exactly when to go, October and March are a good compromise: dark enough evenings for a solid chance at the aurora, with milder weather and more daylight left over for exploring during the day.

One thing to rule out clearly: the northern lights don't show in Iceland in July. Around the summer solstice, Iceland gets close to 24 hours of daylight, so the sky never gets properly dark — no amount of solar activity changes that. Tours don't resume until late August, once the nights start drawing in again.

Why Book a Northern Lights Tour Instead of Going Alone

You can technically go looking for the aurora on your own with a rental car, but most visitors get far better results with a guided tour, for a few practical reasons:

Guides track live forecasts. Aurora strength and cloud cover both change by the hour. A local guide checks both in real time and drives to wherever the sky is actually clearest that night — not just wherever the itinerary says.

You're not driving unfamiliar roads in the dark. Winter driving conditions in rural Iceland can be genuinely difficult for visitors unfamiliar with ice, wind and unlit roads. Letting someone else drive removes a real safety concern.

Most tours offer a safety net. Many of the tours we recommend include a free second attempt or an open ticket if the lights don't appear on your scheduled night — something you don't get chasing them solo.

Frequently Asked Questions

📍 What is the best place to see the Northern Lights in Iceland?
Anywhere away from city light pollution with a clear, dark view works, but the two most practical bases are Reykjavik, with the widest choice of tours and nightly departures, and Akureyri in North Iceland, which offers darker skies and far fewer crowds. Other strong spots include the Snaefellsnes Peninsula and the south coast, usually reached as part of a guided tour.
🌌 When are the Northern Lights visible in Iceland?
The northern lights are visible in Iceland whenever the sky is dark enough, which runs from late August to mid-April. They are not visible in summer, since Iceland's near-24-hour daylight in June and July means the sky never gets fully dark.
🗓️ What is the best time to see the Northern Lights in Iceland?
The best window is late September through March, with November, December and January offering the longest, darkest nights and the most viewing time each evening. Clear, moonless nights within this period give the strongest odds.
✈️ What is the best time to visit Iceland for the Northern Lights?
Most travellers get the best balance of dark skies and manageable winter travel conditions by visiting between October and March. December and January have the darkest skies but also the harshest weather and shortest daylight hours, so shoulder months like October, November and March are worth considering if you also want to sightsee during the day.
☀️ Do the Northern Lights show in Iceland in July?
No. Iceland's near-midnight sun means the sky doesn't get dark enough to see the aurora in June or July, regardless of solar activity. Northern Lights tours pause for the summer and resume in late August as the nights start to darken again.
🚐 Should I book a Northern Lights tour or try to see them on my own?
A guided tour is the easier and more reliable option for most visitors. Local guides track live aurora activity and cloud-cover forecasts and drive to whichever spot has the clearest sky that night, which is very difficult to replicate without a rental car and local knowledge of the area.
🧳 Do Iceland vacation packages include a Northern Lights tour?
Some all-inclusive Iceland vacation packages include a Northern Lights excursion as one of several activities, but many don't, or only include a single basic departure. It's worth checking the exact tour included, or booking a specific Northern Lights tour separately so you can choose the operator, group size and itinerary yourself.
