Why Sweden Is One of the Best Places to See the Northern Lights

When people ask where the best places to see the northern lights are, the obvious answers are Norway and Iceland — but Sweden consistently outperforms both when it comes to one thing that actually matters above everything else: clear skies.

Abisko's microclimate is genuinely exceptional. Lake Torneträsk and the surrounding Scandinavian mountain range create a pocket of stable, dry air over Abisko that deflects the cloud systems affecting the rest of Lapland. The result is an average of more than 45 clear nights per season in the prime viewing window — a figure that most other Arctic destinations simply can't match. When aurora hunters talk about the best spots to see the aurora borealis in Europe, Abisko comes up first, not as a compromise, but as the actual answer.

The auroral zone is directly overhead. Swedish Lapland sits right in the middle of the auroral oval, the band of sky where geomagnetic activity is most intense. On a night with even moderate solar activity, the lights here can be extraordinary.

The infrastructure is world-class. From the Aurora Sky Station above the clouds to cosy wilderness lodges and well-run small-group tours, Swedish Lapland has built a proper northern lights viewing ecosystem that makes for a seamless experience whether you're a first-timer or a veteran aurora hunter.

Abisko — The Best Spot to See the Aurora Borealis in Europe

If there's one place on this site we'd send a friend who only had a single week and one chance to see the northern lights, it's Abisko. The case for it is simple: more clear nights, darker skies, and a dedicated infrastructure built around one thing — giving you the best possible view of the aurora borealis.

The Aurora Sky Station, perched on Mount Nuolja above the town, can be reached by chairlift and sits above the cloud layer on many nights when the ground is overcast — a feature that's essentially unique in the world of northern lights viewing. Even on nights when the cloud rolls in, the station often has a clear window overhead while the valleys below are grey.

Tours from Abisko tend to be small-group, personal and focused — guides here know the local sky intimately and rarely settle for a mediocre viewing spot when a better one is minutes away. If you're serious about seeing the northern lights, Abisko is where to start — see our full Abisko tour comparison.

Kiruna — Swedish Lapland's Gateway to the Northern Lights

Kiruna is Swedish Lapland's largest city and the region's main transport hub, with direct flights from Stockholm making it the most practical entry point for most travellers. It sits well above the Arctic Circle, firmly in the auroral zone, and offers a strong selection of northern lights tours alongside a wider range of Arctic activities — from dog sledding and reindeer safaris to overnight stays at the world-famous ICEHOTEL.

The sky over Kiruna is darker and clearer than almost any city of its size, and the surrounding wilderness is just minutes from the centre — meaning most northern lights excursions here don't need to drive far before the city lights drop away entirely. It's a great base if you want the full Swedish Lapland experience alongside your aurora viewing, rather than focusing entirely on the lights. See our full guide to Kiruna northern lights tours.

When Is the Best Time to See the Northern Lights in Sweden?

The northern lights season in Swedish Lapland runs from late September to late March, whenever the nights are dark enough. Within that window, the best time to go for the northern lights is November through February, when darkness sets in by mid-afternoon and tours have the most viewing hours each evening. For a full month-by-month breakdown across every destination, see our best time to see the northern lights guide.

A few things to plan around:

Peak aurora activity: The equinoxes — late September and late March — tend to coincide with heightened geomagnetic activity, making them favourite times for aurora hunters who want to maximise their chances of a strong display.

Northern lights best time to view each night: Activity tends to peak between 10pm and 2am, though displays can appear any time after dark. Most tours run in this window.

Moon phase matters: A new or crescent moon gives the darkest possible sky and makes fainter aurora much more visible. It's worth checking the lunar calendar when choosing your specific dates.

When is the best time to view the aurora borealis overall: In Swedish Lapland, any clear night between mid-November and early February is excellent — dark, cold, and with long enough nights that even late-appearing aurora won't cut your viewing short.

How to See the Northern Lights in Sweden

There are essentially two ways to approach northern lights viewing in Swedish Lapland: join a guided tour, or go independently. Here's the honest comparison — for the complete step-by-step process, see our full guide on how to see the northern lights.

Guided northern lights tours are the right choice for most visitors. Local guides track live aurora forecasts, KP index readings and satellite cloud maps and drive or lead groups to whichever spot has the best combination of clear sky and dark horizon that night. The best guides in Abisko and Kiruna have years of experience reading the local weather patterns and know which valleys clear first and which hills give the widest view. Thermal suits, hot drinks and photography help are usually included, so you're not standing in the Arctic cold fumbling with camera settings.

Going independently is possible if you have a rental car, know how to read aurora forecast apps like Space Weather or My Aurora Forecast, and are comfortable driving on icy Arctic roads at night. The upside is flexibility — you can stay out as long as you like and go wherever the sky looks clearest. The downside is that without local knowledge, you'll likely miss the spots that guides know from experience rather than apps.

For most travellers, especially first-timers, a guided tour from Abisko or Kiruna is the better bet. The cost is worth it when you consider the alternative: standing in the cold next to your hire car, staring at a partly cloudy sky, hoping for the best.
