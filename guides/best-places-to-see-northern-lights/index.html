The Best Places to See the Northern Lights

Where are the northern lights, and where can you actually see them? We've ranked every major aurora destination by sky clarity, tour quality, accessibility and what the experience is actually like — so you can choose the right one for your trip.

See All Destinations

The northern lights appear across a surprisingly large part of the northern hemisphere every winter — but "appearing" and "being seen" are two very different things. The difference between a life-changing night under the aurora and a disappointing one spent staring at a cloudy sky almost always comes down to one thing: where you're standing.

The aurora borealis is most reliably visible within the auroral oval — a band of maximum aurora activity that encircles the polar region at roughly 65–72° North latitude. (If you want to understand exactly what the northern lights are, they are the result of charged solar particles hitting Earth's magnetic field). Within that oval, certain locations have dramatically better clear-night records, better infrastructure and better guides than others. Choosing between them isn't obvious, and most travel sites give you a vague list without telling you why each place is different or who it's actually right for.

We've spent years comparing these destinations properly — not just by latitude, but by average clear nights per season, tour quality, group sizes, accessibility and value for money. Below is our honest, detailed breakdown of the best places to see the northern lights, ranked and compared. If you want to know exactly how to see the northern lights successfully, picking the right destination from this list is your most important first step.

What Makes a Great Northern Lights Destination?

Before the rankings, it's worth understanding what actually separates the best spots to see the aurora borealis from the merely good ones. Four factors matter most:

Position in the auroral oval. The closer you are to the auroral oval, the more often the lights are active overhead. All the destinations on this list sit within or just below it — but the ones that rank highest tend to be deepest inside it, where even moderate solar activity produces visible displays.

Clear sky frequency. This is the factor most people overlook. A destination can sit perfectly inside the auroral oval and still be a disappointing choice if it's under cloud most of the time. Arctic weather is notoriously unpredictable, and local microclimates vary enormously. Abisko in Swedish Lapland, for example, gets significantly more clear nights per season than most of its neighbours — a fact that's well-known among serious aurora hunters but rarely mentioned in mainstream travel content.

Light pollution. City glow washes out faint aurora displays. The best viewing spots are always away from town centres, which is why guided tours almost always drive you 20–60 minutes into the countryside before stopping. The remoter your base, the less driving you need to do to find proper darkness.

Infrastructure and tour quality. A great location is wasted without knowledgeable guides, reliable transport and a backup plan for cloudy nights. The best aurora destinations have all three — guides who watch live forecasts and drive to wherever the sky is clearest, and operators who include a free second attempt if the lights don't appear.

The Best Places to See the Northern Lights

Ranked by overall aurora experience — clear sky frequency, tour quality, accessibility and what the night actually feels like.

🥇 #1 Best for Clear Skies

Abisko, Sweden

🌤️ 45+ clear nights/season 📍 68°N Swedish Lapland

The single best spot to see the aurora borealis in Europe, and arguably the world. Abisko's extraordinary microclimate — created by Lake Torneträsk and the surrounding mountain range — deflects the cloud systems that affect the rest of Lapland, giving it more clear nights per season than almost any other Arctic destination. The Aurora Sky Station above the town sits above the cloud layer on many nights, making it the only viewing spot in the world with a built-in advantage over bad weather.

🥈 #2 Best for Tour Variety

Tromsø, Norway

🌌 Auroral oval centre 📍 69°N Northern Norway

The world's most popular northern lights destination — and deservedly so. Tromsø sits directly beneath the auroral oval at 69° North, with more tour operators, more nightly departures and more variety than anywhere else on this list. From budget bus chases to luxury fjord cruises and small-group photography expeditions, no other city offers this range. Guides here are world-class, and the city itself is worth the trip regardless of whether the lights appear.

🥉 #3 Best for Extreme Experience

Svalbard, Norway

🌑 24hr polar night Nov–Jan 📍 78°N High Arctic

There is nowhere on Earth quite like Svalbard for the northern lights. Between November and February, this Norwegian archipelago halfway between mainland Norway and the North Pole experiences polar night — 24 hours of complete darkness — giving the aurora more hours to appear than anywhere else in the world. Polar bears outnumber people here. You reach viewing spots by snowmobile, dog sled or boat, through a landscape so empty and vast it barely feels real. If you want the most extraordinary northern lights experience that exists, this is it.

⭐ #4 Best for Dark Skies & Quiet

Alta, Norway

🏔️ World's first aurora observatory 📍 70°N Finnmark

Alta calls itself the City of the Northern Lights — and it has earned the name. Home to the world's first Aurora Borealis Observatory, it sits deep inside the Arctic Circle in Finnmark, Norway's most remote county, with far less tourist traffic than Tromsø. The result is darker skies, smaller groups, and a viewing experience that feels more personal. Alta also offers one of the most distinctive tour formats on this list: a snowmobile night adventure that takes you out into the Arctic wilderness before the sky lights up. If you want Norway's aurora without Norway's crowds, Alta is the best spot.

⭐ #5 Best for Accessibility

Iceland

✈️ Direct flights from US & Europe 📍 64–66°N North Atlantic

Iceland is the most visited northern lights destination in the world — not because it's the best for pure aurora odds, but because it's the most accessible. Direct flights from North America and Western Europe land in Reykjavik, and the combination of dramatic landscapes, midnight hot springs and the aurora borealis overhead makes for one of the most complete winter travel experiences anywhere. For aurora viewing specifically, Akureyri in the north offers darker skies and fewer crowds than Reykjavik, and both cities have excellent tour options including Golden Circle day trips, premium small groups and yacht cruises from the harbour.

⭐ #6 Best for Photography

Svolvær, Lofoten Islands

📸 Most dramatic scenery 📍 68°N Northern Norway

If you want a photograph of the northern lights that looks like nothing else — jagged mountain peaks rising from frozen fjords, fishing villages lit orange against a green sky, the aurora reflected in the water below — Svolvær in the Lofoten Islands is where you come. It sits at the same latitude as Tromsø with equally strong aurora activity, but the scenery it frames the lights against is in a completely different league. Tours here run in smaller groups than in the bigger cities, making it a favourite among serious aurora photographers and anyone who wants more than just a sighting.

⭐ #7 Best for Full Lapland Experience

Kiruna, Sweden

🏨 Home of the ICEHOTEL 📍 68°N Swedish Lapland

Kiruna is Swedish Lapland's largest city and most connected base — direct flights from Stockholm make it easy to reach — with a strong northern lights tour selection alongside the broader Arctic experience that most travellers want alongside the aurora. Dog sledding, reindeer safaris and overnight stays at the world-famous ICEHOTEL all run from here. The aurora viewing itself is excellent, and the surrounding wilderness means guides never need to drive far to find dark skies. A great choice if you want more than just the lights from your trip.

⭐ #8 Best for Igloo Stays

Finnish Lapland

🛏️ Glass igloo capital of the world 📍 66–68°N Finland

Finnish Lapland — centred on Rovaniemi and Levi — has turned northern lights tourism into an art form. The region is the undisputed home of the glass igloo experience: a private heated cabin with a transparent roof that lets you watch the aurora from your bed. It's a completely different way to experience the lights compared to standing in a field in the cold, and it's wildly popular for a reason. The aurora viewing is slightly less reliable than Abisko or Norway due to more variable cloud cover, but for the overall experience — especially for couples or first-timers who want comfort alongside the spectacle — Finland is hard to beat.

How to Choose the Best Northern Lights Destination for You

With eight strong options above, the honest answer is that the best place to view the northern lights depends entirely on what you want from the trip beyond the lights themselves. Here's how to narrow it down:

If you want the highest odds of a sighting above everything else: Go to Abisko. The microclimate advantage is real and documented. No other destination in Europe gets as many clear nights per season.

If you want the widest choice of tours and the most flexibility: Go to Tromsø. Nightly departures, every format from budget bus to luxury cruise, and guides who've been doing this for decades.

If you want the most extraordinary, once-in-a-lifetime experience: Go to Svalbard. Polar night, dog sleds, polar bears and the aurora overhead for 24 hours. Nothing else comes close for sheer drama.

If you want the aurora plus a full travel experience: Iceland gives you waterfalls, geysers, hot springs and the northern lights in one trip — the most rounded winter destination on this list.

If you want Norway's aurora without Norway's crowds: Alta is the answer. Deeper inside the Arctic Circle, fewer tourists, and a snowmobile adventure you won't find anywhere else.

If the photograph matters as much as the sighting: Svolvær and the Lofoten Islands frame the aurora against the most dramatic scenery of any destination on this list.

If you want to watch the aurora from your bed: Finnish Lapland and its glass igloos are the only place on this list where that's genuinely possible.

Where Are the Northern Lights Located?

The northern lights are located wherever charged particles from the sun — travelling along Earth's magnetic field lines — collide with gas molecules in the upper atmosphere at an altitude of roughly 100–300km above the surface. That interaction releases energy as light, producing the aurora.

This happens most intensely within the auroral oval, a doughnut-shaped zone centred on the magnetic North Pole that sits at roughly 65–72° North latitude. The destinations on this page all sit within or just below this zone, which is why they see the northern lights so regularly. Move significantly below it — to, say, 55° North — and aurora displays become rare events rather than regular occurrences, dependent on unusually high solar activity to push the oval southward.

When to See the Northern Lights (Across All Destinations)

The season varies slightly between destinations, but as a general rule the northern lights are visible wherever the nights are dark and long enough — which runs from late August to late March or mid-April across most of the locations on this page.

The best overall window is late September to February, with the core months of November, December and January giving the most viewing hours per night. December and January are peak season for most operators, with the longest nights and the most hours of darkness for the aurora to perform in.

The equinox periods — late September and late March — are also worth noting. Geomagnetic activity tends to increase around the equinoxes, meaning stronger and more frequent aurora displays on nights that happen to be clear.

One near-universal rule: the northern lights cannot be seen in summer from most of these destinations. Midnight sun in Scandinavia and Iceland means the sky never gets dark enough, regardless of solar activity. Tours and meaningful sightings don't resume until late August at the earliest. Frequently Asked Questions

🌟 Where is the best place to see the northern lights?
The single best place depends on what you're optimising for. For the most reliable clear skies, Abisko in Swedish Lapland is the top pick — its microclimate gives it more clear nights per season than almost anywhere in the Arctic. For the widest choice of tours, Tromsø in Norway leads. For the most extraordinary experience, Svalbard during polar night is unmatched. For the best overall travel experience alongside the lights, Iceland is hard to beat.
📍 Where are the northern lights located?
The northern lights are most reliably visible within the auroral oval — a band of maximum aurora activity that sits at roughly 65–72° North latitude, encircling the polar region. The best viewing destinations — northern Norway, Iceland, Swedish Lapland, Finnish Lapland and Svalbard — all sit within or just below this zone.
🔭 What is the best spot to see the aurora borealis in Europe?
Abisko in Swedish Lapland is widely regarded as the single best spot to see the aurora borealis in Europe, due to its exceptional microclimate and the Aurora Sky Station above the town. Tromsø in northern Norway is the most popular overall choice, with more tours and infrastructure. Both sit firmly within the auroral oval.
👁️ Can you see the northern lights from anywhere?
The northern lights are regularly visible only from high-latitude locations within or near the auroral oval, roughly above 60° North. During very high solar activity (KP8-9), they can occasionally be seen from much lower latitudes including the UK and central Europe, but these events are unpredictable and rare. If seeing the lights is your goal, book a trip to one of the destinations on this page during the season.
🇳🇴🇮🇸 Which is better for northern lights — Norway or Iceland?
Both are excellent. Norway — particularly Tromsø and Alta — tends to have more tour variety and stronger aurora reliability due to its position deeper in the auroral oval. Iceland is more accessible from North America and Western Europe and offers a broader travel experience. If you only care about aurora odds, Norway edges it slightly. If you want a richer overall winter trip, Iceland is exceptional.
🗓️ When is the best time to go to see the northern lights?
The best overall window across all major destinations is late September through February, with November, December and January offering the most viewing hours each night. The equinox periods (late September and late March) also tend to bring heightened geomagnetic activity. Summer is not viable from most destinations due to midnight sun.
🚗 Where can I see the northern lights without a tour?
You can see the northern lights independently in any aurora destination with a rental car and the right forecast tools — apps like Space Weather Live show real-time KP readings. However, local guides dramatically improve your odds by knowing which specific viewpoints have the clearest sky that night. Most first-time visitors get significantly better results on a guided tour.
