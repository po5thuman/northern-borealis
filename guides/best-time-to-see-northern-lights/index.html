The Best Time to See the Northern Lights

When can you see the northern lights — and when should you actually go? A month-by-month guide to every major destination, so you can plan around the odds rather than just the calendar.

See the Month Guide

Timing a northern lights trip is equal parts science and luck — but the science part you can actually control. Knowing when the nights are longest, when geomagnetic activity is statistically highest, and when each destination is at its best is the difference between a trip built around real odds and one built around hope.

The honest answer to "when is the best time to see the northern lights" is any clear, dark night between late September and late February — with some nuances by destination, month and what else you want from the trip. The aurora itself doesn't follow a strict timetable. It appears when solar activity is high enough and the sky is dark and clear enough to see it. What you're really planning around is maximising the number of evenings where both those things are true at the same time.

This guide breaks that down properly — by month, by destination, and by the things that actually move the odds in your favour.

⚡ The short version: If you can only read one thing, it's this — November through February is the best overall window across all major destinations, with the longest nights and highest tour frequency. December and January give you the most viewing hours per night. Late September and late March can produce exceptional displays due to the equinox effect. Summer is a write-off at most destinations.

What Actually Determines the Best Time to See the Northern Lights

Three independent factors combine to determine whether you see the northern lights on any given night. Understanding them separately makes it much easier to plan:

1. Darkness. This is the most controllable factor. The aurora is always there — the question is whether the sky is dark enough to see it. In Scandinavia and Iceland, that means being there when the nights are long. The further north you go and the deeper into winter you travel, the more hours of darkness you have each evening. December and January give you the most — sometimes 18–20 hours of darkness at the highest latitudes.

2. Clear skies. You can be in the perfect location with a raging aurora overhead and see nothing if there's cloud cover. This is the hardest factor to predict more than a few days in advance, and it's the main reason a guided tour — with a guide who tracks live satellite cloud maps and drives to clear patches of sky — dramatically outperforms going it alone. It's also why destinations with strong clear-sky records (Abisko in Swedish Lapland being the most extreme example) have such a strong reputation among serious aurora hunters.

3. Solar activity. The aurora is powered by charged particles from the sun. The stronger the solar wind and the higher the geomagnetic activity (measured on the KP index from 0 to 9), the more intense and widespread the aurora. You can't plan for this months in advance, but you can track it in the days before your trip using apps like Space Weather Live or My Aurora Forecast, and adjust where you go each evening accordingly.

The Equinox Effect — Why September and March Can Be Exceptional

One of the most reliably overlooked facts in northern lights planning is that the aurora is statistically more active around the equinoxes — late September and late March — than at almost any other time of year, including midwinter.

This happens because of the way Earth's magnetic field aligns with the solar wind around the spring and autumn equinoxes. The geometry allows solar particles to penetrate the magnetosphere more easily, producing stronger and more frequent geomagnetic storms — and stronger northern lights displays. It's a well-documented pattern in space weather data and consistently shows up in long-term aurora records.

What this means practically: a clear night in late September or late March has a good chance of producing a stronger display than a clear night in December, even though December has the longest darkness. The trade-off is that September and March also have shorter viewing windows each night — fewer hours of darkness means fewer hours for the aurora to perform in. The best of all worlds is a long, dark, clear equinox night — which is rare but extraordinary when it happens.

How Long Do the Northern Lights Last?

This is one of the most common questions we get, and the honest answer is: it varies enormously. Here's what the range actually looks like:

A brief flicker: During moderate activity (KP3-4), the aurora might appear as a pale green arc for a few minutes, fade, then reappear elsewhere in the sky. These shorter displays are more common and still beautiful, but less dramatic than the videos most people have seen online.

A full display: On stronger nights (KP5-6), the aurora can be active for one to two hours, moving across the sky, changing colour and shape, and occasionally exploding into the rippling curtains of light that appear in most aurora photographs. These nights happen several times per season at most destinations.

An exceptional night: During high geomagnetic activity (KP7+), displays can run for three to five hours continuously, sometimes with the lights filling the entire sky from horizon to horizon. These events are rarer but genuinely unforgettable when they coincide with a clear sky.

The reason most guided northern lights tours run for three to four hours is precisely to cover this range — giving you the best chance of being outside when the lights appear, for long enough to see a proper display if they do.

Best Time to See the Northern Lights — By Destination

While the general season applies across all major destinations, each has its own nuances. Here's what to know for each one:

🇳🇴 Norway — Tromsø, Alta, Lofoten, Svalbard

Season: Late September – Late March · Peak: November – February

Norway's aurora season runs from late September to late March across Tromsø, Alta and the Lofoten Islands. Svalbard is the exception — polar night there runs from late October to mid-February, giving it effectively 24 hours of darkness per day and the longest potential viewing window of any destination in the world. For mainland Norway, November through February is the sweet spot for both darkness and tour frequency. See our full Norway northern lights guide with tours from Tromsø and Alta.

🇮🇸 Iceland — Reykjavik, Akureyri

Season: Late August – Mid April · Peak: October – February

Iceland has the longest aurora season of any major destination — late August to mid-April — because of its slightly lower latitude compared to mainland Scandinavia. The best time to visit Iceland for the northern lights is October through February, with November and December giving the most viewing hours. October is particularly good value: dark enough evenings for excellent aurora odds, milder weather than midwinter, and plenty of daylight left for sightseeing. Compare tours from Reykjavik and Akureyri.

🇸🇪 Sweden — Abisko, Kiruna

Season: Late September – Late March · Peak: November – February

Swedish Lapland follows a similar season to Norway, with the main window running from late September to late March. What sets it apart — particularly in Abisko — is the clear-sky frequency rather than the season length. Abisko's microclimate gives it significantly more clear nights per season than most of its neighbours, meaning the best time to go there is whenever you can get a booking — good odds hold across the entire season. Kiruna follows the same seasonal pattern and is slightly more accessible, with direct flights from Stockholm. See our full Sweden guide.

🇫🇮 Finland — Finnish Lapland, Rovaniemi, Levi

Season: Late September – Late March · Peak: November – February

Finnish Lapland's season mirrors Norway and Sweden, running from late September to late March. Finland is the home of the glass igloo experience, and most igloo hotels are fully booked months in advance for December and January — so if that's your plan, booking early matters more than timing flexibility. The shoulder months of October and March often have availability at shorter notice and can offer excellent displays. See our full Finland guide and Finnish Lapland tours.

When Is the Best Time to View the Aurora Borealis During the Night?

Within any given evening, aurora activity tends to peak between 10pm and 2am local time, though displays can begin earlier and last until dawn. This peak window corresponds to when Earth's magnetic field is optimally positioned relative to the solar wind — a pattern that holds reasonably consistently across all major aurora destinations.

Most guided northern lights tours depart between 8pm and 10pm and run until midnight or 1am, covering the core of this window. If you're going independently, plan to be outside and in a dark location from 9pm onwards on nights with a good forecast. Checking the KP index hourly and watching for sudden upticks in activity is more reliable than trying to predict exactly when the display will begin.

⭐ Tips for maximising your timing

  • Book for at least 3 nights minimum — a single night leaves you entirely dependent on that one night's weather
  • Check cloud cover forecasts, not just aurora forecasts — a KP7 night under complete cloud is worthless
  • The equinox windows (late Sept, late March) often produce the strongest displays — don't write them off as shoulder season
  • December and January have the most darkness hours, but that doesn't automatically mean better displays
  • New moon nights give the darkest skies and make faint aurora much more visible — check the lunar calendar
  • Book a guided tour — guides track both aurora and cloud forecasts live and drive to clear sky

When Do the Northern Lights Occur?

The northern lights occur continuously — the aurora is an ongoing physical process driven by the solar wind interacting with Earth's magnetic field, every single day of the year. What changes is whether the sky is dark enough to see them, and how intense the solar wind is on any given day.

At its most basic, the aurora occurs when charged particles from the sun travel along Earth's magnetic field lines toward the poles and collide with gas molecules in the upper atmosphere at roughly 100–300km altitude. The collisions release energy as light — mostly green (from oxygen at lower altitudes), but also red, purple, blue and white at different heights and intensities.

This process correlates with the 11-year solar cycle. During solar maximum — when the sun is most active — aurora events are more frequent and more intense. We are currently in or approaching Solar Cycle 25's maximum, which is expected to produce some of the most active aurora conditions in over a decade through the mid-2020s. If there was ever a time to book a northern lights trip, this is it.

🌞 Solar Cycle 25 — why 2025 and 2026 are exceptional years: The current solar cycle is approaching its predicted maximum, meaning solar activity — and therefore aurora frequency and intensity — is at its highest point in roughly a decade. Several record-breaking geomagnetic storms have already occurred this cycle. The window of elevated activity is expected to continue through 2026, making the next two aurora seasons among the best in recent memory.

Frequently Asked Questions

🗓️ When is the best time to see the northern lights?
The best time to see the northern lights across all major destinations is late September through February. The core peak is November, December and January — when nights are longest and darkest. The equinox periods (late September and late March) often produce stronger displays due to heightened geomagnetic activity.
📅 What is the best time of year to see the northern lights?
Winter — specifically November through February — is the best time of year. This is when nights are longest, skies are darkest, and aurora tours run most frequently across Norway, Iceland, Sweden and Finland. October and March are strong shoulder months with milder conditions. Summer is not viable due to near-24-hour daylight at most destinations.
👁️ When can you see the northern lights?
The northern lights can be seen from late August through mid or late April at most major aurora destinations in Europe, whenever the sky is dark and clear enough. In Svalbard, polar night means potential 24-hour viewing from late October to mid-February. Outside this window at other destinations, midnight sun prevents sightings regardless of solar activity.
⏱️ How long do the northern lights last?
A single display can last from a few minutes to several hours. Brief flickers of 5–15 minutes are common during moderate activity. Strong displays (KP5-6) often run for one to two hours. Exceptional nights with very high geomagnetic activity (KP7+) can produce continuous aurora for three to five hours or longer. Most guided tours run three to four hours to cover this range.
🌌 When do the northern lights occur?
The aurora occurs continuously as a physical process, but can only be seen at night in dark skies. Activity tends to peak between 10pm and 2am local time. It correlates with solar activity and the 11-year solar cycle — we are currently near Solar Cycle 25's maximum, meaning conditions are particularly favourable through 2026.
🌙 When is the best time to view the aurora borealis during the night?
The aurora borealis is most often active between 10pm and 2am local time. Most guided tours are structured around this window. On nights with very high activity, displays can begin earlier in the evening and continue until dawn — but on a given clear night, planning to be outside from 9pm onwards covers the core window well.
🌍 Is the northern lights season the same in all destinations?
Broadly yes, but with slight variations. Iceland's season starts earliest (late August) and ends latest (mid-April). Mainland Scandinavia — Norway, Sweden, Finland — runs from late September to late March. Svalbard is the exception, with polar night providing a 24-hour viewing window from late October to mid-February.
