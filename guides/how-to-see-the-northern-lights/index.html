How to See the Northern Lights

You've decided you want to see the aurora borealis. Now what? This guide covers everything — location, timing, forecasts and the honest truth about whether a tour is worth it.

See the Full Guide

Seeing the northern lights for the first time is one of those experiences that people describe as life-changing — and also one that a surprising number of travellers come home disappointed from. Not because the aurora didn't appear, but because they were in the wrong place, at the wrong time, looking at a cloudy sky.

The good news is that the difference between those two outcomes is almost entirely controllable. Knowing how to see the northern lights properly — not just showing up somewhere cold and hoping for the best — comes down to seven things, and we're going to cover all of them.

⚡ The honest short version: Get to a high-latitude destination (Norway, Iceland, Sweden or Finland) between late September and late March. Go out on clear nights between 10pm and 2am. Get away from town. Check the KP index before you leave. Book a guided tour if it's your first time. That's it — everything else is detail.

How to See the Northern Lights — 7 Steps

Choose a destination inside the auroral zone

The northern lights are most reliably visible within the auroral oval — a band at roughly 65–72° North latitude where geomagnetic activity is strongest. The major European destinations within this zone are northern Norway, Iceland, northern Sweden and Finnish Lapland. All of them work; the differences between them come down to clear-sky frequency, tour quality and what else you want from the trip.

If maximum aurora odds are the priority, Abisko in Swedish Lapland has a unique microclimate giving it more clear nights per season than almost anywhere else in the Arctic. For the widest tour choice, Tromsø leads. For the most complete travel experience, Iceland is hard to beat. See our full best places guide for a proper comparison.

Travel at the right time of year

The northern lights are active year-round — but you can only see them when the sky is dark. That rules out summer at every major European destination, where midnight sun keeps the sky too bright even at 2am. The season runs from late August to mid-April in Iceland, and late September to late March in Norway, Sweden and Finland.

The best overall window is November through February, when nights are longest and you have the most viewing hours each evening. The equinox periods (late September and late March) often produce stronger than average displays due to heightened geomagnetic activity. For a full month-by-month breakdown, see our best time guide.

Get well away from light pollution

This is the step most people underestimate. Even a small town produces enough light pollution to wash out a faint aurora display — and faint displays are far more common than the dramatic ones that appear in most photos. You need to be in genuinely dark countryside to see the lights properly, not just outside the hotel.

In practice, that means travelling at least 20–40 minutes from the nearest town by car. Most guided tours do this automatically — it's one of the main reasons they produce better results than standing in a hotel car park and staring upward. If you're going independently, use a light pollution map to identify genuinely dark zones near your accommodation before you go.

Check the aurora forecast — understand the KP index

The KP index is the single most useful number in aurora planning. It runs from 0 to 9 and measures geomagnetic activity globally — the higher the number, the stronger and more widespread the aurora. Here's what each level means at major European destinations (roughly 65–70° North):

KP IndexWhat it means at 65–70°NVerdict
KP 0–1Very low activity — aurora faint or invisible even with clear skiesPoor
KP 2Faint aurora possible very close to magnetic pole onlyMarginal
KP 3Visible aurora on clear nights at all major destinationsGood
KP 4Solid display, good colour and movement likelyVery good
KP 5Strong display, visible lower in the sky, wider geographic rangeExcellent
KP 6–7Dramatic display — active curtains, strong colour, horizon to horizonOutstanding
KP 8–9Major geomagnetic storm — aurora visible from much of EuropeExceptional

The best apps for tracking the KP index in real time are Space Weather Live, My Aurora Forecast and Aurora Alerts. All three offer push notifications when the KP spikes above a threshold you set — useful if you want to be woken at 1am when activity suddenly increases.

Watch the cloud cover separately

The aurora forecast and the weather forecast are two completely different things — and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes first-time aurora chasers make. A KP9 night under thick cloud is completely useless. A KP3 night with a crystal-clear sky is wonderful. Cloud cover is equally important as solar activity.

Check a dedicated weather app — Yr (Norwegian meteorological service, excellent for Scandinavia), Windy (shows cloud layer forecasts on a map), or the local weather service for Iceland (Vedur.is) — alongside your aurora forecast. What you're looking for is clear skies, no cloud, and a KP of 3 or above simultaneously. When those two things align, go out.

Go out between 10pm and 2am

Aurora activity tends to peak between 10pm and 2am local time, aligning with when Earth's magnetic field is optimally positioned relative to the solar wind. This doesn't mean the lights never appear outside this window — they can, and sometimes a display begins at 8pm or runs until 4am — but if you're only going out once, this is the window to be in.

Plan to stay outside for at least two to three hours on any night with a good forecast. Aurora displays are rarely constant — they come and go in waves. A brief clear spell with visible lights might fade after ten minutes, then return dramatically an hour later. Patience matters more than perfect timing.

Dress for the conditions — and plan your photography

Standing outside in Arctic temperatures for two to three hours is a very different experience from a casual winter walk. Dress in proper thermal layers — a moisture-wicking base layer, an insulating mid-layer, and a windproof outer shell. Warm boots, gloves and a hat are non-negotiable. Hand warmers in your pockets extend how long you can comfortably stay out.

For photography: set your camera to manual mode, open the aperture as wide as it goes (f/1.8–f/2.8 ideal), use a shutter speed of 5–20 seconds depending on how active the display is, and set ISO to 800–3200. Mount on a tripod — even a slight shake ruins a long exposure. If you don't have a camera, many guided tours include professional photography of your group under the aurora.

Consider booking a guided northern lights tour

This step is optional — but for most visitors, especially first-timers, it's the one that makes the biggest difference. Here's why: a good local guide does steps 3, 4 and 5 simultaneously, in real time, every night of the season. They track both the aurora forecast and the cloud-cover map, and they drive to wherever the combination is best — sometimes two hours from where the tour started. That adaptability is what separates a sighting from a disappointing night.

Guided Tour vs Going It Alone — An Honest Comparison

Whether to book a guided northern lights tour or go independently is a question worth thinking through properly. Here's the honest breakdown:

✅ Guided Tour — Advantages

  • Guide tracks live aurora and cloud forecasts simultaneously and drives to the best spot
  • No need for a rental car or knowledge of local roads
  • Warm suits, hot drinks and photography usually included
  • Most tours include free rebooking if lights don't appear
  • Safety on winter roads handled for you
  • Guide knows viewpoints that aren't on any map
  • Group experience — often more memorable than solo

⚠️ Going Solo — Things to Consider

  • Requires a rental car — and winter Arctic driving experience
  • You track your own forecasts — easy to miss the cloud picture
  • No rebooking safety net if you pick the wrong night
  • No local knowledge of best viewpoints
  • Standing alone in a field in the cold is different from a group experience
  • Photography help not available unless you bring your own skills
  • Can be excellent for experienced travellers who've done it before

Our honest verdict: if it's your first northern lights trip, book a guided tour. The improvement in odds alone is worth the cost, and the free rebooking policies on most tours mean you're not gambling your entire trip on a single night's weather. If you've done it before, know how to read forecasts and are comfortable with winter driving, going independently is a perfectly valid — and often rewarding — approach.

Northern Lights Viewing Checklist

Before you head out on any aurora night, run through this checklist:

  • KP index checked — KP3 or above forecast for tonight
  • Cloud cover checked — clear or mostly clear skies at your viewing location
  • Destination sorted — at least 20–40 minutes from the nearest town
  • Timing right — heading out between 9pm and 2am
  • Dressed properly — thermal layers, warm boots, gloves, hat
  • Hand warmers in pockets
  • Camera charged, tripod packed (if shooting your own photos)
  • Phone fully charged — for forecast updates and safety
  • Aurora alert notifications turned on on your forecast app
  • Booked a guided tour, or have a plan B if tonight's solo attempt is cloudy

Frequently Asked Questions

🌌 How do I see the northern lights?
Get to a high-latitude destination (Norway, Iceland, Sweden or Finland) between late September and late March. On clear nights with a KP forecast of 3 or above, get away from town and go out between 10pm and 2am. A guided tour significantly improves your odds by combining live aurora and cloud-cover tracking with local knowledge of the best viewpoints.
👁️ How to view northern lights — what do I actually need?
You need: a destination within the auroral zone, dark skies away from light pollution, a clear night, and enough solar activity (KP3+). Warm clothing for several hours outside is essential. A camera on a tripod lets you photograph the display; most guided tours include this as standard.
🚗 How can I see the northern lights without a tour?
You need a rental car, real-time access to aurora and cloud-cover forecasts, and comfort driving on icy roads in the dark. Use Space Weather Live for KP readings and Yr or Windy for cloud cover. Drive 20–40 minutes from town on clear, high-KP nights and find an open dark viewpoint. This works well for experienced travellers; first-timers generally get better results on a guided tour.
📱 What is the KP index and what app should I use?
The KP index (0–9) measures global geomagnetic activity. A KP of 3 or above is generally visible at major European destinations. KP5+ produces strong displays. The best apps are Space Weather Live, My Aurora Forecast and Aurora Alerts — all offer push notifications when the KP spikes.
🌌 Is a guided tour worth it for seeing the northern lights?
For most visitors, yes. Local guides track live forecasts and drive to wherever conditions are best — often two hours away. Tours include thermal suits, hot drinks, photography and usually a free rebooking if lights don't appear. For first-time visitors especially, this dramatically improves the odds of a sighting.
