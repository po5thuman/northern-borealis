What Are the Northern Lights?

The aurora borealis is one of the most extraordinary natural phenomena on Earth — but what actually creates it, why does it appear in different colours, and where does the name come from? Everything you need to know, explained clearly.

Explore the Science

The northern lights — known scientifically as the aurora borealis — are a natural light display produced when charged particles from the sun collide with gases in Earth's upper atmosphere. The result is one of the most breathtaking spectacles in the natural world: curtains and ribbons of green, red, purple and white light rippling silently across a dark polar sky.

They have been watched, wondered at, and written about for thousands of years. Ancient Norse peoples believed they were the light reflected from the shields of the Valkyries riding across the sky. Indigenous peoples across Arctic North America saw them as the spirits of ancestors dancing. Finnish mythology called them "revontulet" — fox fires — after a mythical fox whose tail swept sparks across the heavens as it ran.

Today we understand the physics behind them — and the science, if anything, makes them more extraordinary, not less. This guide covers everything: what the northern lights actually are, what creates them, why they appear in different colours, the different names they're known by, and the facts that most articles get wrong or leave out. Whether you want to know how to see the northern lights or simply understand them better, you are in the right place.

Those collisions release energy as light. The colour depends on which gas is involved and at what altitude. Green comes from oxygen at lower altitudes; red from oxygen at higher altitudes; blue and purple from nitrogen.

What Are the Northern Lights?

The northern lights are a natural atmospheric light phenomenon — not a weather event, not a reflection, and not a product of the moon or stars. They are generated by the interaction between charged particles from the sun and the gases in Earth's upper atmosphere, guided to the polar regions by our planet's magnetic field.

More specifically, the northern lights are a form of plasma emission. When fast-moving electrons and protons from the solar wind collide with oxygen and nitrogen molecules in the ionosphere — the layer of Earth's atmosphere between roughly 60km and 1,000km altitude — those molecules absorb the kinetic energy of the collision and become temporarily excited. As they return to their ground state, they release that energy as photons: particles of light. Billions of these collisions happening simultaneously across a wide arc of sky produce the aurora borealis.

The word aurora comes from the Roman goddess of dawn, Aurora, and borealis is Latin for "northern," derived from the Greek word Boreas, the god of the north wind. The name was first applied to this phenomenon by Italian astronomer and physicist Galileo Galilei in 1619 — though the lights had been observed and written about long before that.

What Creates the Northern Lights — Step by Step

Understanding what creates the northern lights is easier when you break the process into its individual steps. Here is how the aurora borealis is formed, from the sun's surface to the sky above your head:

1

The sun emits the solar wind

The sun constantly releases a stream of charged particles — mostly electrons and protons — called the solar wind. This stream travels outward through space in all directions at speeds of 400–800 kilometres per second. During periods of high solar activity, such as solar flares or coronal mass ejections (CMEs), much denser and faster bursts of particles are released, producing stronger aurora events when they reach Earth.

2

Earth's magnetic field deflects most particles

Earth is surrounded by a magnetic field — the magnetosphere — generated by the movement of molten iron in its outer core. This field acts as a shield, deflecting the vast majority of solar wind particles around the planet. Without it, Earth's atmosphere would be gradually stripped away by the solar wind, as has happened to Mars. The magnetosphere is compressed on the sun-facing side and stretched into a long tail on the night side.

3

Particles are funnelled toward the poles

Near the magnetic poles, Earth's field lines converge and dip down into the atmosphere, creating openings in the magnetospheric shield. Charged particles from the solar wind travel along these field lines, descending into the upper atmosphere in a ring-shaped zone around each magnetic pole — the auroral oval, centred at roughly 65–72° North (and South) latitude. This is why the aurora is most reliably visible from high-latitude locations in Norway, Iceland, Sweden and Finland.

4

Particles collide with atmospheric gases

As the charged particles descend into the ionosphere, they collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms at altitudes of approximately 100–300km. These collisions transfer energy to the atmospheric atoms, temporarily boosting electrons within those atoms to higher energy levels — an excited state. The specific altitude of the collision and the type of gas involved both affect what happens next.

5

Excited atoms release light

When an excited atom returns to its ground state, it releases the absorbed energy as a photon — a particle of light. The wavelength of that photon (which determines its colour) depends on how much energy was released, which in turn depends on which atom was excited and at what altitude. Billions of these emissions happening simultaneously across the auroral zone produce the aurora borealis visible from the ground.

Northern Lights Colors — What Creates Each One

One of the most common questions about the aurora borealis is why the northern lights appear in different colours. The answer lies in which gas is being excited and at what altitude in the atmosphere the collision is occurring. Here is a breakdown of each colour and what produces it:

Green

The most common aurora colour by far. Produced by oxygen molecules at altitudes of roughly 100–150km. The oxygen atom absorbs energy from a colliding particle, becomes excited, and releases a green photon as it returns to ground state. Green is the colour most people associate with the northern lights and the one most visible to the naked eye.

Red

Produced by oxygen at much higher altitudes — above 200km, where the atmosphere is extremely thin. At this altitude, oxygen atoms stay in their excited state for longer before releasing energy, producing a lower-energy, longer-wavelength photon: red. Red aurora appears as a fringe above strong green displays and is less commonly seen, partly because it's near the edge of human visual sensitivity.

Purple & Violet

Produced by nitrogen molecules at lower altitudes, typically at the base of auroral curtains. Nitrogen emits at several wavelengths simultaneously, creating the blue-purple fringing often visible at the lower edge of a strong display. Violet aurora is more commonly visible in photographs than to the naked eye.

Blue

Also produced by nitrogen, particularly ionised nitrogen (N₂⁺) at lower altitudes. Blue aurora is relatively rare and faint compared to green, and tends to appear at the lower edge of intense displays. It is more commonly captured in long-exposure photography than seen with the naked eye, which is less sensitive to blue wavelengths in low-light conditions.

Yellow & White

Not produced by a single gas, but by the blending of green (oxygen) and red (oxygen) emissions occurring simultaneously. When green and red overlap in a display of sufficient intensity, the eye perceives the combination as yellow or white. True white aurora is rare and indicates an unusually strong, multi-altitude event.

Pink

A blend of red oxygen emission (high altitude) with the blue-purple from nitrogen (low altitude), or occasionally a mixture of all three primary aurora colours appearing at the same time. Pink aurora often appears at the lower edge of a very strong display and is one of the rarer and more spectacular colours to witness in person.

Names for the Northern Lights — Around the World

The aurora borealis is known by many different names across languages and cultures. The scientific name — aurora borealis — was coined by Galileo Galilei in 1619, combining the name of the Roman goddess of dawn with the Latin word for northern. But peoples across the Arctic and sub-Arctic had their own names and stories for the lights long before that.

The equivalent phenomenon in the southern hemisphere — visible from Antarctica, southern South America, New Zealand and the southern tip of Australia — is called the aurora australis, or southern lights. The physics are identical; only the location differs. Together, the two polar light displays are known as the polar auroras.

Facts About the Northern Lights

The northern lights are more remarkable than most people realise. Here are the facts that tend to surprise people most:

🌍

100–300km up

The aurora occurs at stratospheric altitudes — far above commercial aircraft (which fly at 10–12km) and far below the International Space Station (400km), which actually flies through the aurora.

☀️

11-year solar cycle

Aurora activity follows the sun's roughly 11-year cycle of activity. We are currently near Solar Cycle 25's maximum — meaning conditions are among the best in over a decade through 2026.

🌐

Oval-shaped zone

The aurora doesn't appear uniformly around the poles — it appears in a ring-shaped zone called the auroral oval, centred on the magnetic (not geographic) North Pole.

🔊

Sometimes audible

Very strong aurora displays are occasionally accompanied by faint crackling, clapping or hissing sounds. This was long dismissed as myth, but has been confirmed by recordings made during strong geomagnetic storms.

🧲

Disrupts technology

The same geomagnetic storms that produce spectacular aurora can disrupt GPS signals, radio communications, power grids and satellite systems. The strongest storms on record (like the 1859 Carrington Event) caused telegraph systems to catch fire.

🔭

Named in 1619

The term "aurora borealis" was first applied to the phenomenon by Galileo Galilei in 1619, though the lights had been observed and written about by ancient Greeks, Romans and Indigenous peoples for thousands of years before that.

🪐

Not just on Earth

Aurora also occur on other planets with magnetic fields, including Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. The Hubble Space Telescope has photographed spectacular auroras on Jupiter, far more powerful than anything seen on Earth.

🌙

Invisible in summer

The aurora is active year-round, but cannot be seen from most high-latitude locations in summer because the sky never gets dark enough. In Scandinavia and Iceland, midnight sun prevents sightings from roughly May to August.

📷

Brighter in photos

Cameras are more sensitive to green light than human eyes in low-light conditions. Aurora photographs — especially long exposures — typically show more vivid colour and detail than the same display looks in person.

Frequently Asked Questions

🌌 What are the northern lights?
The northern lights — scientifically known as the aurora borealis — are a natural light display caused by charged particles from the sun interacting with gases in Earth's upper atmosphere. Funnelled to the polar regions by Earth's magnetic field, these particles collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms at altitudes of 100–300km, releasing energy as light and producing the colourful, moving displays visible in the night sky.
⚡ What creates the northern lights?
The northern lights are created by the solar wind — a stream of charged particles emitted by the sun — being channelled toward Earth's magnetic poles by the planet's magnetic field, where the particles collide with atmospheric oxygen and nitrogen. Those collisions excite the atoms, which then release the absorbed energy as photons of coloured light. The colour depends on which gas is involved and at what altitude the collision occurs.
🎨 What makes the northern lights different colours?
Each colour is produced by a different gas at a different altitude. Green comes from oxygen at 100–150km. Red comes from oxygen at higher altitudes (200km+). Purple and blue come from nitrogen. Pink is a blend of red and blue-purple. White or yellow aurora is a mix of green and red. The most commonly seen colour is green, because oxygen is most abundant at the altitudes where most aurora activity occurs.
📛 What is the scientific name for the northern lights?
The scientific name is aurora borealis — Latin for "northern dawn," coined by Galileo Galilei in 1619. The equivalent in the southern hemisphere is called the aurora australis. Together they are called the polar auroras. In everyday language they are also called the northern lights, the aurora, or in some languages: nordlys (Norwegian), norrsken (Swedish), revontulet (Finnish) and norðurljós (Icelandic).
🔬 How are the northern lights formed?
The northern lights are formed when solar wind particles travel along Earth's magnetic field lines toward the poles and enter the upper atmosphere. There, at 100–300km altitude, they collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms, exciting them to a higher energy state. When those atoms return to their normal state, they release the absorbed energy as visible light — the aurora borealis.
📚 What are some interesting facts about the northern lights?
Key facts: the aurora occurs 100–300km above the surface; green is produced by oxygen, red by oxygen at higher altitude, blue-purple by nitrogen; aurora activity follows an 11-year solar cycle; strong displays can occasionally be heard as faint sounds; the same geomagnetic storms that create aurora can disrupt GPS and power grids; aurora also occur on Jupiter, Saturn and other planets with magnetic fields; and cameras capture aurora colours more vividly than the naked eye due to their greater sensitivity to green light.
🌍 What other names are the northern lights known by?
The northern lights are known as aurora borealis (scientific Latin), nordlys (Norwegian/Danish), norrsken (Swedish), revontulet (Finnish), norðurljós (Icelandic), aurora boreale (Italian), aurore boréale (French) and aurora boreal (Spanish). The Sami people of Arctic Scandinavia call them guovssahasat — meaning roughly "light that can be heard." The southern equivalent is the aurora australis.
