Why Chase the Northern Lights from Akureyri

Akureyri, Iceland's capital of the north, is a completely different aurora experience from the busy tour routes around Reykjavik. Here's what makes it worth the trip:

It's genuinely remote. Akureyri sits at the head of Eyjafjörður, surrounded by mountains, fjords and open countryside. Guides don't need to fight traffic or crowds to find a dark, clear spot — they're usually just minutes away.

Far fewer people make it this far north. Most visitors to Iceland never leave the Reykjavik–Golden Circle–South Coast loop, which means Akureyri's tours run in smaller groups, at quieter viewpoints, without the elbow-to-elbow scramble for a good spot.

The photos are genuinely different. No crowds in the frame, no light pollution washing out the color, and — on the photography tour — a guide whose entire job is getting you a shot worth printing, not just a phone snap in the dark.

Best Time to See the Northern Lights in Akureyri

The Akureyri, Iceland aurora season runs from late August to mid-April, in line with the rest of the country. The best window is late September through March, with November, December and January offering the longest, darkest nights. North Iceland's winter air also tends to be clearer and drier than the south coast, which can mean better visibility on a given night even when the forecast looks similar.

Since fewer tours run here than in Reykjavik, and the best small-group options are limited-capacity, it's worth booking a few days ahead if your travel dates are fixed — especially for the photography tour.
