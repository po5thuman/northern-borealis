There's a particular kind of quiet that settles over an Iceland northern lights tour just before the sky lights up. The bus stops somewhere dark, engines switch off, and everyone steps out into the cold and looks up. Then — sometimes in minutes, sometimes after an hour of patient waiting — a pale green curtain starts to move across the stars.

Iceland is one of the most accessible places on Earth to chase the aurora borealis. Reykjavík sits close enough to the Arctic Circle that on a clear, dark night, you have a genuinely strong chance of a sighting — but "clear" is the key word. Iceland's weather flips fast, and clouds over the capital don't mean clouds everywhere. That's exactly why a guided tour beats going it alone: your guide watches live cloud cover and aurora forecasts and drives wherever the sky is darkest and clearest that night.

We've compared the six best Northern Lights tours and excursions departing Reykjavík below — from budget-friendly bus trips to a full Golden Circle day — so you can pick the one that fits your trip and book with confidence.

Why Take a Northern Lights Excursion in Iceland?

Iceland is consistently ranked among the best places to see the northern lights — and after chasing the aurora here ourselves, we understand why. Here's what makes a guided Iceland northern lights tour worth it:

Reykjavík is a genuinely good base. You're close enough to the Arctic Circle for strong aurora activity, and a short drive gets you away from the city's lights entirely. On clear nights during peak season, sightings are common.

The weather changes fast — guides adapt. Icelandic skies can be clear in one valley and overcast twenty minutes away. A local guide checks live cloud-cover and aurora forecasts and reroutes on the fly, something that's nearly impossible to do on your own without a rental car and local knowledge.

You're looked after. Hot cocoa, warm blankets, hand warmers and included photography mean you can focus on watching the sky instead of worrying about logistics.

Best Time to See the Northern Lights in Iceland

The Iceland northern lights season runs from late August to mid-April, whenever the sky is dark enough. The best window for most travellers is late September through March, with November, December and January offering the longest, darkest nights and the most hours of viewing time each evening.

If you're wondering whether the northern lights show in Iceland in July — they don't. Iceland's near-midnight sun means the sky never gets properly dark in June or July, so tours pause for the summer and pick back up once the nights start drawing in around late August.

Planning your trip around the best time and place to see the northern lights in Iceland? Give yourself at least three or four nights in the country if you can — it dramatically improves your odds of catching a clear one. Frequently Asked Questions

🗓️ What is the best time to see the Northern Lights in Iceland?
The season runs from late August to mid-April. The best window is late September through March, with the longest, darkest nights — and the best odds — in November, December and January.
☀️ Do the Northern Lights show in Iceland in July?
No. Iceland's near-24-hour daylight around the summer solstice means the sky doesn't get dark enough to see the aurora in June or July, regardless of solar activity. Tours resume in late August as the nights darken.
📍 What is the best place to see the Northern Lights in Iceland?
Anywhere with a clear, dark view away from Reykjavík's city lights works, including Þingvellir, the Snæfellsnes Peninsula and the south coast. Most visitors get the best results by joining a guided tour, since guides track live forecasts and drive to whichever spot has the clearest sky that night.
⏰ Do I need to book an Iceland Northern Lights tour in advance?
Yes. The best-rated small-group and full-day tours from Reykjavík often sell out during peak season (October–March), especially on weekends and around holidays. Booking a few days to a few weeks ahead is a good idea.
✨ Are Northern Lights tours in Iceland worth it?
Yes, for most travellers. Local guides track live aurora and weather forecasts and drive away from clouds and light pollution — something very difficult to replicate without a car and local knowledge. Many tours also offer free rebooking if the lights don't appear.
